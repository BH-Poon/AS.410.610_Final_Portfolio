Introduction

The concept of genetics was spotted within science throughout history before the experiments by of Augustinian friar Gregor Mendel on trait inheritance in pea plants rooted the topic as a scientific focus in the early to mid-20th century. Within the mid-20th century, the transformation of bacterial cells by Oswald Avery, Colin MacLeod, and Maclyn McCarty reoriented genetics towards inheritable blueprints present in cells, with the infamous double-helix structure deduced by Rosalind Franklin in coordination with experiments from James Watson and Francis Crick. The work of numerous scientists, the fraction of which were mentioned here, provided a basic understanding of genetics and the knowledge on the allusive blueprint for life, DNA, which leads to the next question: How do we read this blueprint? Early attempts began with reading protein sequences by Frederick Sanger in 1955, followed by RNA sequences by Walter Fiers et al. Reading DNA sequences came towards the late 20th century through a method developed by Sanger and employed strategies developed by other scientists. The method, in turn, sequencing of the first DNA genome- bacteriophage ?X174- in 1977, and was widely adopted to become the first-generation in DNA sequencing[1].

This highly condensed history in genetics and DNA created the environment for a new field of biology, as it was analogous to the soil Mendel used and the field of bioinformatics were the seeds. Although the birth of bioinformatics may be debatable based on the respondent, the water used to germinate the seeds of bioinformatics was largely supplied by the legacy of the Human Genome Project (HGP). The HGP was one of the greatest feats in scientific history, encompassing dozens of institutions (with much more scientists) at a cost of over USD$2 billion over the course of a decade, aimed at sequencing and mapping the Homo sapiens genome[2]. Although the actual project has been criticized for its goals, the project's legacy led to technological advancements for sequencing technology, which exponentially increased the amount of genomic information available and drastically reduced the cost. Reports by the National Institutes of Health (NIH) indicated that costs of sequencing a human genome have fallen below $1000 after 2015[3]. 

The cost-effective sequencing, combined with advancements in sequencing platforms, leads to the abundance of genomic information produced, and the rate of production, which leads us here to the rise of bioinformaticians needed to analyze and interpret that information. Bioinformatics is the child of two parents: computer science and biology. The former parent provides the tools for storing and gathering data, while producing statistical tools to create algorithmic programs to meet needs. The latter parent provides the biological knowledge to logically establish relationships, determine functions, and interpret results from the former parent. In a nutshell, bioinformatics is an interdisciplinary field dedicated to the pursuit of biological molecular investigations utilizing computer science methodologies. As this field touches the corners of modern biological and physical science, I, too, hope that this learning tool can touch upon your appreciation for this field. This tool is designed for students with science backgrounds that are pursuing a future in bioinformatics. As such, some of the topics may not be explicitly explain upon and will require personal learning.
