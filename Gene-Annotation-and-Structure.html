GenBank Records 

GenBank is a genetic sequence database, hosted by the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI), that is part of the International Nucleotide Sequence Database Collaboration, which includes the DNA DataBank of Japan (DDBJ) and the European Nucleotide Archive (ENA)[1]. GenBank records provides submitted information on genes, transcripts, and proteins that are tied to a unique accession number. Information can include a locus identifier, definition, date of updated versions, molecular origin (mRNA, DNA, or protein), length of the sequence, organism, comments, and publications. The GenBank record can also provide raw sequences in FASTA format or link to a web-based genome browser through the Graphics option. The most important part of any GenBank record utilized by a bioinformatician is the features table. The features table summarizes the protein or DNA record with annotations, or “features”, of the transcript/sequence in a tabulated-format that includes sequence-specific information and (if applicable) external references to outside databases. These sequence-specific information can be segments of the sequence, such as introns, exons, and the coding sequence (CDS). The CDS annotated is the region of DNA that encodes the protein product. The CDS contains the coded exons only, in which case there will be no 5’ UTR, 3’ UTR, or poly-A. Other features included in the table are promoters, poly-A, repeats, and exons. Refer to here for more information on formats for annotations. Keep in mind the directionality of the strand sequence. DNA is read from 5’ to 3’ direction on the plus strand, but sequences can be found on the minus (or complement) strand. It will be indicated if the sequence is on the minus strand with operators. The operator for complimentary strand is “complement(<location>)”. Another operator is for joining non-adjacent parts of sequences (i.e. when introns are spliced out). Refer to 3.4.2.2. of the documentation link referred earlier for more location operators. 


[1] https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/genbank/      

A challenge with GenBank record annotations is that only the original authors can submit updates to the GenBank record, unless expressed permission from the original authors were given to GenBank for the update. Further annotation updates to the record by a non-author can only be performed by sending experimental or inferential data to GenBank’s Third-Party Sequence Annotation (TPA) database. This poses a challenge in quality of records that results in GenBank being a redundant and non-curated database, which allows multiple submissions of sequences already present in the database. A non-author will be able to submit their data from their sequence even though the same sequence has a GenBank record. This redundancy also adds another layer to the lack of reliability and confidence. Albeit one can always defer to the newest update/submission as a method to filter through the noise, it is still the least ideal way to get the most updated record. As the database gets older, further questions may come to mind that: What if all the original authors were not able to be contacted? Take for example if they were all deceased. Would there be another submission record from another author be made for the original annotations to update any inaccurate annotations? Is there a de facto waiting period for contacting original authors before this is done? There is a beauty in redundancies, but in several decades (or even years) the database may become too overwhelmed with archaic data.   

Annotation and Prediction

Annotation and Prediction After sequencing, first focus is usually on prediction and annotation of the sequence, especially with that of the CDS. Prediction is the first step. Prediction often takes advantage of similarities between known genes/proteins and models are made by aligning to known genes/proteins. Gene prediction algorithms have been used in conjunction with automated annotation programs to newly sequenced (full or partial) genomes. There are annotation pipeline methods (i.e. MAKER and MAKER2) that use RNA-seq and other data for annotations. Pipeline methods do make mistakes and finding errors are difficult. There are tools (i.e. WebApollo) that help with reducing mistakes. Refer to this article by Yandell and Ence (2012) on eukaryotic gene annotation for examples of challenges with regards to splice variants.
