Eukaryotic Gene Prediction

As discussed before under the chapter Gene Annotation and Structure, eukaryotic genes are monocistronic with the ability to produce alternative protein products due to splicing. Although this layer of complexity does add to the ability of predicting and annotating eukaryotic genes, many programs for eukaryotic gene predictions are designed with this in mind. We will see here that some of the gene prediction programs employ statistical methods and sensors to increase accuracy. Eukaryotic gene prediction looks for exons. The first exon begins with start codon because the 5’ UTR, 3’ UTR, and non-coding exons are usually ignored by programs. In this case, the first coding exon begins at the location where the start codon is adjacent to the splice donor. Conversely, the last coding exon ends at where the stop codon is adjacent to the splice acceptor. 

Gene prediction can have small-scale or large-scale goals. The approach largely depends on the goal. An example of a small-scale goal can be finding or confirming the exonic location of a gene. A large-scale goal can be to identify undiscovered genes. A wholistic approach to predicting genes is by utilizing comparative genomics with related species. The caveat here is that species must be relatively close, but distant. For example, a chimpanzee genome would not be well-suited for comparing with a human genome because the introns and non-coding DNA are almost as conserved as the coding DNA. Likewise, a yeast genome is too distant from a human genome in that the coding regions are not well conserved in evolutionary distance (as well as the lack of yeast homologs). The middle ground would be to use a mice genome that has the right level of conserved regions in the coding exons. Comparative genomics can be used in addition to de novo prediction in order to improve the prediction accuracy.      

De Novo Prediction Methods

Eukaryotic gene predictions consist of two main types of methods for prediction: De novo or expression-based. Expression-based methods align the cDNA and protein sequences to genomic sequences to find the CDS, as such the gene structures predicted by expression-based methods are very accurate. However, the limitation is that expression-based methods require the use of cDNA in the form of expressed sequence tags (ESTs) that may not be available for certain genomes. Simultaneously, another limitation stems from the inherency that 20% to 40% of most genomes code for rare genes, which makes generating ESTs even harder. De novo (also known as Ab initio) gene prediction programs analyze genomic sequences and predict from sequence information only without referring to a genomic sequence. Programs for de novo gene prediction include: GENSCAN, HMMGene, FGENESH, and Augustus. GENSCAN, developed at Standford and hosted at MIT, utilizes three categories of probability in prediction of exons. The first category range starts from very high probability (p > 0.99), which are almost always correct and are used with confidence for creating PCR primers. The second category range is moderate to high (0.50 < p < 0.99), which are still quite accurate. The third category range is low probability (p < 0.50), which are not reliable and should be ignored (or treated with caution). The benefits of GENSCAN include a very organized output that includes FASTA-formatted sequences. However, the predictions do not work very well with lower vertebrates, because it was designed for human and vertebrae DNA. Interestingly, it does predict internal exons better than the first and last exons, which is a normally expected outcome. HMMGene, hosted by the Center for Biological Sequence Analysis (CBS), is a prediction program that utilizes signal options for prediction of splice sites, start codon, and stop codon. The program allows for a top-3 prediction rather than a “best prediction”, which gives it a slower run time. HMMGene is designed for human and C. elegans DNA, but is acceptable when used for all vertebrates. Since de novo programs do not require ESTs, they succeed where expression-based methods fail due to those limitations.  

Expression-based Prediction Methods

Expression-based prediction methods align the sequenced cDNA to a known genome in order to extrapolate the CDS and UTRs, which are part of the cDNA. Programs for expression-based methods include Splign, genomic BLAST, and the BLAST-like Alignment Tool (BLAT). It is of interest to note that all three tools mentioned here are hosted by NCBI. Splign is used to align cDNA to a specific genomic sequence that must be added into the program. The cDNA portion is for the predicted or unknown sequence, while the Genomic portion is for the segment of reference genome. Genomic BLAST uses ESTs, which are essentially low-quality partial cDNA, to estimate the alignment to a reference genome. BLAT uses an input from the predicted or unknown sequence and aligns it to a reference genome assembly. It is similar to Splign, but vary in not having to provide the reference genome, which adds ease of use, and not having a graphical output.

Prediction algorithms can contain additional tools that help improve its prediction accuracy. One such tool is the inclusion of content sensor predictions that use inherent features of the coding sequence, such as base composition. An example of this is the bias against the third base in bacterial codon that are assumed to be more random in non-coding DNA than coding DNA due to selective pressure. One of the most reliable content sensors is hexamer frequency, which uses Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) with codon bias based on the organism and di-codon combinations. The HMM is generated from training sets used for determining probability in using hexamer patterns through known genes to find unknown genes. Another content sensor is a signal sensor program, which finds genes based on sequence that is associated with the gene. Examples include signals for promoters, splice sites, stop codons, and poly-A tails. Signal sensors find signals using Position-Specific Scoring Matrices (PSSMs) that give more weight to conserved regions than non-conserved regions. It is important to note that HMMs can also be used to score signal sensors. A popular tool to employ signal and content sensors is the use of neural networks with a training set, which is not based on probability.
