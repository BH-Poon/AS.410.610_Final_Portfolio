RNA And Ultraconserved Regions

RNA

RNA are more than the transcripts that are usually considered as the “middle-man” between genes and proteins. One of the most critical roles they play was not strongly known, nor appreciated, until early in the 21st century and the role is noncoding RNA (ncRNA). Although there are still much to learn about ncRNA, we do have much appreciation for their ability in fine-tune regulation of gene expression. However, the term ncRNA is defined as RNA molecule transcripts that do not translate into protein and broadly encompasses multiple classes of noncoding RNA: transfer RNA (tRNA), ribosomal RNA (rRNA), long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs), small nucleolar RNA (snoRNA), small nuclear RNA (snRNA), microRNA (miRNA), short interfering RNA (siRNA), and guide RNA (gRNA). Even today, more classes of ncRNAs are being discovered. However, omitted from the list here are messenger RNA (mRNA), which are translated into protein. Compared to previously believed, studies show that lncRNA may be more ubiquitous with possible roles in transcriptional regulation, epigenetics, and brain function.    

A new term that may be used synonymously with ncRNA is the term functional RNA (fRNA). However, fRNA is broader, encompassing both ncRNA and functional secondary structures in mRNAs. Some examples of fRNA that are not ncRNAs include riboswitches and the selenocysteine insertion sequence (SECIS) element. One program focused on RNA is PPfold, which can accept multiple alignments of RNAs and uses phylo-SCFG methods focused around entropy to predict RNA folding, as described by Sükösd et al. (2013). 

RNA Prediction and Resources

Some tools for predicting miRNA targets include: miRanda, PicTar, and DIANA-TarBase v.8. The algorithm miRanda finds miRNA and miRNA targets on an mRNA by looking for target sites on the 3’ UTR using comparative genomics. The PicTar algorithm was described in a Nature Genetics article and has pre-calculated predictions for miRNA. The DIANA-TarBase v.8 algorithm predicts miRNA-to-mRNA targeting, with the input for either miRNA or mRNA needed, and includes links to external sources. 

Some databases that contain information for ncRNA include miRDB, miRBase, TargetScan, miRNEST 2.0, PolymiRTS, NONCODE, and Rfam. The TargetScan database contains precalculated miRNA predictions that are separated by species and gene symbol. The miRNEST 2.0 database contains data from deep sequencing libraries and predictions of animal and plant miRNA. PolymiRTS contain data on SNPs in miRNA target sites for both known and predicted sites, in which case a SNP located at an important base-pair will interfere with miRNA-based gene silencing. The miRDB is a database that allows searching of a given mRNA to return miRNA actors, and vice versa. The miRBase is a database for known miRNA sequences, with detailed records, annotation, and data from deep sequencing reads. The NONCODE database is a manually curated database with records of lncRNA transcripts from lncrnadb for over 500,000 sequences across 16 species. Rfam is a database hosted by the European Bioinformatics Institute (EBI) that is a ncRNA-based version of Pfam. Rfam includes information on structure and function for ncRNA categorized into Families, Clans, Motifs, and structure.  

Ultraconserved Elements

The term Ultraconserved Element (UCE) was first described in 2004 by Bejerano et al. as regions of over 200 base-pairs that have 100% identity with no gaps in human, mouse, and rat- the identity and length of which cannot be statistically attributed to chance. While many of these UCEs are not found in coding regions, the UCEs found in non-coding regions were referred to as “gene deserts”. Incidentally, the 100% identity requirement for UCEs ensure that SNPs are extremely rare, even though the variant may not be lethal. Another interest to note about UCEs is that they are rarely detected outside of chordates, which show how conserved they are in evolutionary terms. UCEs may have functions attributed to them, such as uc322, which was found to be an intron of the SOX6 gene that appears to be an enhancer in melanocytes. Many discovered UCEs were linked to tumorigenesis were attributed to a new class of ncRNAs called transcribed ultraconserved regions (T-UCR), indicating the delicate responsibilities that these gene regions have in the organism. 

Ultraconserved Elements and Conserved Noncoding Elements

The terms UCEs and T-UCRs are very restricting in their definitions. In which case, there are two other terms for a conserved, but less stringent region. The first is called long conserved noncoding sequences (LCNS) and are defined as sequences longer than 500 base-pairs with more than 95% identity. The second is highly conserved noncoding sequences (CNEs) that is more of a modern focus with less limitations, as described by Harmston et al. in 2013, by defining CNEs based on the pair of species that are compared against each other. 

Tools for researching CNEs include the databases: ANCORA, cneViewer, UCNEbase, and CEGA. The ANCORA database has a genome browser for 11 species to compare with each other with the option of downloading the data or exporting the tracks to the UCSC Genome Browser or Ensembl. In addition, cneViewer is a genome viewer that displays conserved regions between only humans and zebrafish. Comparatively, UCNEbase contains records of over 4,000 ultraconserved non-coding elements (UCNEs), a new term used by Dimitrieva and Bucher to describe most conserved non-coding elements, across 18 species and is more advanced/comprehensive when compared to other current databases. CEGA is a newly established database with data on similarly-based regions using another term: conserved non-coding sequences (CNCs). At the end of this paragraph, one must think that there is numerous terminology all focused around the same conceptual idea, with specific parameters given that altercate what encompasses one and not the other. What is the order? Will it change? It’s not surprising those questions comes up in your mind. This topic, as well as the database and resources that accompany it, are new, relative to the date of this being written. Compared to other subjects, the research on these conserved (whether ultra or not) are still minimal and the data is young. These terms and resources will change by the time you are reading this. However, this tool may not be outdated- it’s just the start to helping you understand the history behind this topic.
