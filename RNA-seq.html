RNA sequencing (RNA-seq)

RNA-seq

When compared to the previous chapter on ChIP-seq, RNA-seq employs the same initial principle of obtaining a target of interest and, more often than not, aligning the sequence data to a reference genome. In RNA-seq, the target of interest is the transcriptome of the sample. The transcriptome encompasses all the cellular RNA transcripts, such as mRNA, tRNA, ncRNA, etc. RNA-seq analyses reveals locations, and levels, of known and novel transcripts by providing quantitative information on gene expression across cell types and sample conditions. New transcripts expressed under rare conditions are still being discovered. The uses for RNA-seq are enormous in that it can define transcript start sites, reveal new ncRNAs, find alterative splice products or sites, and- most importantly- quantitatively estimate transcript levels. The latter is the most important in that this particular attribute to RNA-seq, as well as improvements in technology that reduces the cost, ultimately results in RNA-seq replacing traditional microarrays. Microarrays were widely used, until recently, to measure the relative gene expression of the cell due to the background noise generated, but fails to quantitatively measure the gene expression. Comparatively, RNA-seq has the resolution of a single base-pair with relatively low background noise. Microarrays are good for general RNA expression levels, but still need very specific probes on the array and the resolution is not adequate for detecting differential splicing expression.Financially, RNA-seq is more expensive with a cost of $200-$300 per sample, but the benefit is that it produces quantitative data of every cDNA that can be sequenced. Comparatively, expression microarrays can only be used for approximately 20,000 known genes and splice variants, with a cost of about $50-$200 per sample for statistically sound results. 

Contrary to the name, RNA-seq is the term for massive parallel sequencing of cDNA- not the RNA. This is because the RNA themselves are the templates for a cDNA library to convert the RNA to cDNA, which is used by NGS platforms to sequence the reads. Following similar principles, the sequenced reads are then aligned to a reference genome to determine the location of the original RNA transcript. Unless the RNA transcript is foreign, such as with RNA-based viruses, the transcript must have been transcribed from a genomic location on the DNA. Reference-Based RNA-seq

There are many tools available in performing reference-based RNA-seq analysis. Most RNA-seq experiments aim to understand the transcriptome of the sample. In which case, comparative analysis of differential expression of the transcriptome between two samples is the central theme. The most commonly used, and well-known, is the TopHatsuite, which includes a Bowtie alignment program as part of the tools. The RNA-seq analysis pipeline can be summarized in five major steps, albeit certain steps may be repetitive upon itself. Given that we have the sequenced reads of a sample under two different conditions, the first step in differential RNA expression analysis is to use the two reads as the input to TopHat, which will map the reads using the Bowtie aligner with an exon-based approach. The mapped reads of each condition will become the input into the Cufflinks tool, as step two, which assembles the reads into transcripts using a probabilistic model. A general transfer format (GTF) file that contains information on gene location and names can be incorporated with Cufflinks to attach those features in the GTF file to the transcripts. The outputs from Cufflinks are two GTF files, with two tables showing gene expression or transcript expression levels. The third step is to use Cuffmerge to incorporate the two files of mapped transcripts into a final, single transcriptome assembly. The output of Cuffmerge goes into Cuffdiff in step four for statistical analysis of differential expression between the two conditions. The mapped reads outputted from TopHat must be inputted with Cuffdiff and the transcriptome assembly for comparison. The result from Cuffdiff is the results of the analysis. Lastly, in step five, the differential expression results from Cuffdiff goes into CummeRbund to be plotted for visually examining the differential expression results. Please see the figure below for the general pipeline of RNA-seq differential expression analysis. Although this is not the third step, and is not shown in the figure, the Cuffcompare tool can be used prior to Cuffmerge to compare the two GTF files from Cufflinks. The goal of comparing may be to compare gene expression levels between different cell types, different developmental stages, or even different disease states. The TopHat suite of tools are available on Galaxy.   

Differential Expression and De novo Assemblers

Challenges can occur when aligning cDNA, that is specific to aligning cDNA and do not occur with genomic DNA sequencing. Most sequenced eukaryotic cDNA reads are from spliced mRNA. Thus, a single read can span an exon-exon boundary with a large gap in alignment from the intron that is missing. This is especially evident with paired-end sequencing when paired reads could appear very far apart on the reference genome. The major challenge lies in assembling the reads into a transcript. This is a unique feature of RNA-seq alignment programs that specializes in overcoming these challenges.   

  As we approach the more modern-day methods and technology, we must keep in mind that new technologies, approaches, and methods for analyzing RNA-seq data are continuously emerging. One new tool is the MATS package, which is a statistical analysis method for the hypothesis testing of alternative splice patterns detected by RNA-seq. Another tool is DiffSplice, which recently found new alternative splicing events that were not reported to the RefSeq or UCSC Genes databases. Diffsplice compares the transcriptomes assembled by multiple RNA-seq datasets to find novel splice transcripts. DESeq2 is another tool that is popular for use in differential expression analysis that is available through Bioconductor. EdgeRis another tool that can be found in Bioconductor. StringTie is a tool developed at Johns Hopkins University that performs transcript assembly and expression analysis in a single step, thereby potentially replacing Cufflinks. Regardless of the tool, they all have one commonality: they rely on the RNA-seq reads to be aligned to a reference genome. 

  What is the reference genome is not available? We have covered situations in NGS where de novo alignment had to be performed for newly sequenced genomes. RNA-seq alignment programs also have de novo assemblers that can be used before transcript assembly and quantification. Similar to de novo NGS aligners, RNA-seq de novo assemblers will align the RNA-seq reads to each other based on overlapping segments to determine what the full RNA molecule must have been. One popular de novo assembler for RNA-seq is Trinity. However, these tools tend to be newer than reference-based assemblers and are actively being developed.   

Single-cell RNA-seq

In addition to the general RNA-seq, a new and growing approach is single-cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq). As the name implies, the goal of this method is to measure the transcriptome of a single cell using SMRT-seq library preparation method. For comparison purposes, a single human cell has at least 20,000 genes and by doing RNA-seq on a single cell, the result can show the expression of at least 20,000 genes. Advancements in NGS technology allows RNA-seq to be performed once, without the number of reads being an issue. The first step to scRNA-seq is to insert a specific barcode sequence, which is usually 12 base-pairs long, in order to determine the origination of the sequence read. A common method of incorporating the barcode sequence into a single cell is through Drop-seq, which encapsulates a cell, and a bead with the barcode sequence attached, in an aqueous droplet that is formed in an oily environment within a microfluidic channel. The cellular membrane is ruptured for a few seconds with detergent and the mRNAs in the cell are captured with the polyA part of the arms in the bead. Lastly, the cDNA is generated using the SMRT-seq protocol mentioned prior. 

In addition to containing the same cell-specific barcode sequence in the arms of the bead, it also contains a unique molecular modifier (UMI) that is different amongst each arm of the bead. The length of the UMI has since been elongated to 16 base-pairs as of 2021. After Drop-seq is performed, paired-end sequencing performed for scRNA-seq using Illumina. The first read is for cell barcode and UMI, while second read is for cDNA. After the reads are sequenced, they are sorted based on the barcode sequence in the first read before the second sequence read is mapped. The number of UMI mapped to each gene is calculated and saved as a count matrix, with each columns representing a single cell and rows representing the genes. The count matrix will contain many “0” values, which means no expression. In order to save disk space, the MTX file format is used to only denote non-zero values from the count matrix. The lines in a MTX file format are in X, Y, Z format, with X and Y denoting the matrix coordinate and Z denoting the non-zero value. Lastly, the MTX file has two corresponding TSV files that go with it: one for genes and the other for the barcode sequences. This way, the genes are mapped to the row of the matrix and the barcode sequence for the cell it originates from.  

After obtaining the UMI count matrix, quality control performed to remove noise and multilets. Noise is where no cell gets into a droplet and multilets occur when two or more cells get into droplet. To identify noise, a kneeplot, which is the cumulative plot of the number of UMIs per cell in decreasing order, is used to determine a cutoff value for noise based on when the graph shifts exponentially. This makes the cumulative version of the UMI/cell plot. Afterwards, normalize the number of UMI by dividing total number of UMIs to the number of cells. The normalized values will be very small and will need to be scaled up. Scale the result up by multiplying 10,000, and take either natural log or log2, which is dependent upon the software. Next, perform dimensionality reduction or clustering analysis with Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and, either t-SNE or UMAP. The Seurat program can perform PCA to clusters and visualize by t-SNE or UMAP. This can be used to identify the marker genes per cluster in order to assign cell type. Lastly, perform differential expression analysis for cell-type.    

A potential problem with scRNA-seq is that when library preparation is done for single cell, the reverse transcriptase often gets stalled in the middle. This can cause failures in generating a full-length cDNA. One solution is to use MMLV (Moloney Murine Leukemia Virus) reverse transcriptase, because it adds the CCC trinucleotide when it reaches 5’ end of mRNA template. This would require the use of a PCR handle with the oligonucleotide GGG added, because it is complimentary to the CCC oligonucleotide and only strands with PCR handles inserted can be amplified to generate full-length cDNA.
