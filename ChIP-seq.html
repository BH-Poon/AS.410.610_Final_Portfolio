Chromatin-Immunoprecipitation Sequencing (ChIP-seq)

Histones

Before we begin to discuss Chromatin-Immunoprecipitation Sequencing (ChIP-seq), we will discuss why there is even a need to sequence the materials from a chromatin. The strands of DNA present in each cell are not free-floating in the nucleus, but are compacted tightly in a series of steps. The first key actor in this compaction is the histone protein. Histones are large protein complexes composed of two copies of four subunits, forming an octomer. The first two subunits are Histone 2A (H2A) and Histone 2B (H2B), which form dimers with each other. This gives us two copies of a larger subunit composed of both H2A and H2B. Histone 4 (H4) and Histone 3 (H3) forms a tetrameric structure with both of their copies, to give us one larger subunit. The H3-H4 tetramer forms the core of the histone and combines with the two H2A-H2B dimers to form the complete histone protein.   


The histone protein interacts with DNA to form a nucleosome by wrapping the DNA strands around the histone. Simply put, histones are the proteins, while nucleosomes are the protein-DNA complexes. The nucleosomes then compact further with the help of Histone 1 (H1) in a stage-wise manner to form chromatin. The combination of nucleosomes and H1 results in the chromatosome, which is looped in a circular manner to form the 30nm fiber. This 30nm fiber folds upon itself in loops to form a strand that is the 300nm fiber. The 300nm fiber are compressed and bent to form the 250nm-wide fiber. Notice here that the description implies the radius of the fiber that it is being compacted into. Tight coiling of the 250nm-wide fiber will produce a chromatid. Click on this link for a visual representation of the process.    

Although the steps show a consistent type of histone protein or subunit used, there are several variants of histone proteins. Some variants may be favored compared to others. It is important to note here that the histone variants are not equivalent to histone modifications! However, histone variants themselves have a regulatory role during development. Histone modifications are post-translational modifications (PTM) to the amino acid residues by adding or removing groups, which alters the way DNA interacts with the histone, thereby affecting accessibility for other proteins. There are several well-known modifications, such as methylation, acetylation, and phosphorylation. Some are more recently discovered, such as sumoylation and ubiquitination. The full list of modifications is long and can be amended to in the following years as further research into PTMs are performed. It is also important to note that these modifications are performed on the amino acid residue of the histone and not DNA. Therefore, PTMs of histones are non-DNA based modifiations that cause changes in chromatin patterns. This is the primary focus in the field of epigenetics!  

ChIP-seq

  Now that we know the significance of histones and chromatins, we can continue to understand why ChIP-seq is performed. ChIP-seq is composed of two portions: the chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) and the sequencing (seq). First, we will start with the ChIP portion with the immunoprecipitation process, which is a method of isolating, by precipitation, a protein of interest using an antibody that specifically binds to the protein. In addition to the specification of ChIP antibodies used, these antibodies are also discriminant in targeting their targets. Antibodies used in ChIP can be designed to target histone modifications like H3K4me3, which stands for the tri-methylation (me3) of lysine 4 (K4) on histone 3 (H3). The result of ChIP is the precipitated targets from the antibody that are composed of the histone proteins and DNA bound to it. The second portion of ChIP-seq is to sequence the DNA that were obtained from ChIP using NGS, with the reads from NGS aligned to the reference genome. Regions with large overlaps of reads, which is referred to as a “pileup” of reads, are interpreted as being associated with the histone modification or histone of interest. However, there are also background binding occurring in the process, as no biological action is flawless. Therefore, negative controls must be used with ChIP-seq experiments to ensure that the final result shows peaks that are significant and not the “noise”.   

ChIP-seq Data Analysis

Similar to the output from NGS runs, the raw reads from ChIP-seq are typically stored in a FASTQ file. At times, but less common, it may be stored in FASTA format. There are many publicly available ChIP-seq datasets published in in SRA and the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) databases, both of which are hosted by NCBI. Obtaining the data may be slightly tricky with the SRA database as it requires the SRA toolkit to be downloaded on a Unix server before using the command fastq-dump. Alternatively, Galaxy has a tool to retrieve data from the SRA database through the Download and Extract Reads in FASTA/Q tool.   

After obtaining the ChIP-seq data in FASTQ format, the same process described in the previous chapter can be used to trim, groom, and align the reads to a reference genome. The difference in the pipeline for ChIP-seq analysis is after the alignment is performed. The aligned sequences, either in BAM or SAM file format, are used as inputs for peak calling. Peak calling identifies the “peaks” in the reads that are aligned to a reference genome and indicates which regions of DNA in the reference genome showed association with the DNA from ChIP portion. In turn, the association indicates that the protein of interest used in the ChIP portion is associated with this region of DNA. The output of peak callers is usually in BED or WIG file formats as they encompass multiple interval-spanning features. Unfortunately, there is no “best” peak calling program, as the program that is used should be dependent on a variety of factors. For instance, one may choose a certain program over another based on how well the program can handle the following factors: the presence of replicates, the performance of control experiments, the use of single- or paired-ends, the type of protein that is immunoprecipitated, etc. Generally speaking, common programs that are used include MACS2 and Sicer, both of which are available on Galaxy. ChIP-seq Controls

One of the factors for deciding upon a peak calling program is the type of control experiment utilized in the ChIP-seq experiment. ChIP-seq controls are important because they help distinguish between the noise in the experiment and the low, but meaningful, signals present in the sequenced reads. Consider the case where two reads align with a single mismatch. Is this an error from low-quality or incorrect bases during signaling? Is it a true signal, but present in a low proportion of the cells sampled? Is it just noise? These are questions that a proper control experiment can help resolve, which saves time from having to replicate the experiments that produced unusable data. Concurrently, another way to improve ChIP-seq signal detection is by sequencing and analyzing the control experiment.  
 
The choice of what type of control experiment depends entire on what is being immunoprecipitated and the overall conditions of the ChIP-seq experiment. There are three major types of control experiments: DNA input control, IgG antibody control, and untagged strains. The DNA input control method is generally a good option for ChIP-seq as it recovers DNA that had not been immunoprecipitated, which represents all the DNA available in the experiment. This can help differentiate between what is a significant result and what is simply noise from the experiment. The second, IgG antibody control, is not recommended for ChIP-seq, but is recommended for other experiments. This control method performs a mock ChIP experiment using an antibody that does not bind to nuclear proteins, which results in small amounts of random DNA that are collected at the end of the ChIP portion. However, the small amounts of DNA must be amplified with PCR prior to sequencing, which adds more noise. Lastly, the untagged strains control method helps control for the antibody being used by using an antibody primed to target a tag that is on the protein. Tags can be commonly used tags, such as GFP. This method will also employ an untagged wildtype as the control to ensure that any protein-DNA complex that is caught by the antibody in the untagged wildtype would also appear in the experiment. However, the drawback to this method is that it is not always possible to tag the protein.  

Another goal of ChIP-seq is to know how the protein binding to DNA can change under certain conditions or in different cell/tissue types. This can be done with differential peak calling programs that can compare the difference between two separate sample sets to determine significance. These programs are designed specifically for the purpose of comparing peaks between multiple sample sets that have different conditions. For a full list of differential peak callers, click here. Having control samples and replicate experiments for this goal is important for the comparison program. Another reason, too, is that some differential peak callers require controls and/or replicates.
