Prokaryotic Genes

Prokaryotic mRNA is frequently polycistronic, where one mRNA transcript results in multiple CDSs adjacent to each other. These groups of genes are known as operons that has a single promoter sequence regulating the transcription of several genes. By regulating the transcription of the operon, an RNA polymerase can bind to the promoter and transcribe the entire operon with all the genes on it. There are usually only one or two nucleotides between the stop codon of one gene and start codon of the next gene in a polycistronic mRNA transcript. Prokaryotic promoters will have RNA polymerase bind to regions near the TSS. These regions are at -10 and -35 (relative to the position of the TSS), and are likely to be binding sites for sigma factor. An alpha-subunit on the RNA polymerase binds to AT-rich region near -40 to -60 called the UP element, which are usually found in strong promoters. 
  
Sigma factors are a subunit of RNA polymerase that binds to the promoter. Bacteria mostly use sigma-70 under normal conditions, but during times of heat stress another sigma factor, sigma-32, replaces sigma-70 in RNA polymerase. This causes the RNA polymerase to bind to promoters specific for the genes of heat shock proteins to increase their expression. Also, there are many more sigma factors, such as sigma-28 (Chlamydia) and sigma-E (E. coli). Finding promoters will require the use of training sets from promoters that are known known to bind to a specific sigma factor. The use of intergenic regions of related species to find promoters assumes that base substitutions are limited inside the promoter because it must bind to the sigma factor. Luckily, prokaryotic promoters have consensus sequences and can be found by statistical methods. Another methodic approach is based on weaker stability of DNA double helix at promoters, which is a normal part of promoter melting during transcription.      

How are prokaryotic genomes classified? Prokaryotic genomes can vary drastically between species. Genome sizes can range from 0.5 megabases (Mb) to 10 Mb, with either a circular or linear geometry. The prokaryotes themselves can have disease significances and varying lifestyles that include being extracellular, extremophilic, symbiotic, etc. Prokaryotic organisms can vary significantly in GC content, which is expressed as a percentage with the formula (G+C)/total bases, which may be attributed to the tendency for some organisms to prefer certain codons over others. Although GC content are usually higher in genes, regions that are atypical in GC content for the organism can be due to horizontal gene transfer (HGT) that allows for interspecies transfer of genetic material. By comparison, GC content remains the same during vertical gene transfer when genes are inherited from parental cells. One commonality between all prokaryotes is the presence of 16S rRNA subunit. This commonality allows the use of 16S rRNA to be used for phylogenetic purposes in determining relationships among prokaryotic species. However, it is important to note that this method is not fool-proof and has challenges that will not be discussed here.   

Prokaryotic Gene Prediction

Open reading frame (ORF) are the ways that a sequence can be read. The ORF can vary between six locations from a sequence start site, due to codons being composed of a triplet. The six locations are stepwise patterns around a nucleotide position, moving to the next nucleotide position (with the + or – indicating upstream or downstream). The predicted amino acid sequences from one ORF will result in the sequence being read with a specific nucleotide sequence as the start site for reading sequences. However, the start site does not indicate the starting methionine, as it can start in the UTR. ORF finding is used to find coding regions on prokaryotic genomic DNA. There are (usually) no introns, so the longest open reading frame are the CDSs. The problem is with ORF finding is determining the start codon when there are multiple possibilities in the reading frame. Each AUG triplet can be either a start codon, methionine, or from a non-coding region upstream of the start codon. Gene prediction programs help with finding the most likely CDS regions from ORF finding results. The accuracy of a gene prediction can be enhanced by using comparative genomics, such as performing BLASTx to a protein database. However, one disadvantage is that gene predictors cannot predict exact RNA sequences because it can only distinguish between coding and noncoding DNA. This would not allow it to determine transcription start site (TSS) or transcription termination sites. This challenge can be resolved with RNA-seq experiments that provide information on transcription start/stop sites in additional to gene expression data). As such, RNA-seq experiments are often complimentary to gene prediction to find the TSS that gene prediction cannot. Vice versa, gene prediction provides the CDS regions that RNA-seq is not as capable of finding. Most modern annotation methods will use gene predictors and transcription expression data in order to annotate prokaryotic genes.  

Some programs for prokaryotic gene prediction include: ORF Finder, FGENESB, GLIMMER, GeneMark, and EasyGene 1.2b. ORF Finder, as the name suggests, finds all available genes from each ORF read. The “true” genes are usually the longest ORFs with non-overlapping ORFs. ORF Finder will show the translated amino acid sequence when the ORF is selected.

FGENSB is a web-based online predictor program (also offered as a local download) based on Markov chain models and can automatically annotate the predicted bacterial genes through the GenBank database. This allows FGENESB to predict coding regions, promoters, operons, translation sites, and termination sites.

GLIMMER can find long ORFs using genomic DNA that originates in a FASTA format by utilizing training sets. Training sets are created from a reference genome of the organism and comparing an unknown sequence from that organism.  If the DNA is unknown or a whole reference genome is absent for the organism, then a reference genome from a related organism can be used to create a training set. However, the prediction may not be as strong depending on the relationship between different organisms. For using training sets from related species, the related species must be of an appropriate evolutionary distance. If it is too closely related, this can result in false positives from not fully diverging. If it is too far in relationship, this can result in only highly-conserved elements, such as with promoters, being detected.  

Additional resources for prokaryotic and archaea gene annotation includes tools and databases such as: Seqinr, Pathosystems Resource Integration Center (PATRIC), HaloWeb, the UCSC Archaeal Genome Browser, Greengenes, BPROM, and the Berkley Drosophila Genome Project. Seqinr is a R-based package tool that allows for simple analysis of FASTA-formatted sequences, such as performing base count, sequence lengths, GC content, etc. PATRIC is a database of over 10,000 microbial genomes and allows genome browsing with cross-genome comparisons to find orthologs. HaloWeb is a database maintained by the University of Maryland’s Marine Biology Center that has information limited to 52 species of haloarchaea. The UCSC Archaeal Genome Browser is a separate genome browser from the widely-known UCSC Genome Browser that is specific for archaeal (and some bacterial) genomes. One benefit of this browser is that, like its widely-known counterpart, it is linkable to the Galaxy server. Lastly, Greengenes is a repository of 16S rRNA sequences with tools used for phylogenetic studies to compare (or identify in some cases) organisms. BPROM can be used to find potential sigma-70 promoters with a claimed accuracy of 80%. The Berkeley Drosophila Genome Project can predict promoters by utilizing a Neural Network Promoter Prediction algorithm that must be set for prokaryotes first. Unfortunately, as the field of bioinformatics grows and expands, some of these tools/databases may become outdated.
